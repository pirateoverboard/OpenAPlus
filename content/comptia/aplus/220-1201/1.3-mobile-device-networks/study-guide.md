# Objective 1.3 study guide: Mobile Device Networks

This domain covers the network connection methods in Objective 1.3: cellular
service, cellular generations, Wi-Fi, Wi-Fi calling, hotspot behavior, SIM/eSIM,
Bluetooth pairing, and GPS/location services.

## Scope and source handling

CompTIA 220-1201 Objective 1.3 defines the scope. Professor Messer 220-1201
v1.70 p.4 was used only to validate concept presence and page references. No
new content was generated from the PDF during the domain split.

Exact cellular transfer-rate examples are not carded. OpenAPlus focuses on the
stable operational distinctions learners need to recognize.

## Cellular networks

Cellular service divides geographic coverage into cells served by antennas and
frequencies. A mobile device can use cellular service for voice, data, or both.

Airplane mode disables cellular radios. Some devices also allow cellular data to
be disabled while voice service remains available. For troubleshooting, separate
the question “can the phone make calls?” from “can the phone use mobile data?”

## Cellular generations

3G, 4G/LTE, LTE Advanced, and 5G describe generations of cellular networking.
The exam focus is the trend: newer generations generally provide better data
capability and make richer mobile services practical.

3G improved mobile data enough to support richer services than earlier cellular
generations. LTE is a 4G technology. LTE Advanced improves on LTE. 5G is a newer
generation with higher performance goals and stronger support for large data
transfers, monitoring, notification, cloud processing, and IoT workloads.

## Wi-Fi and Wi-Fi calling

Wi-Fi connects a mobile device to a local 802.11 network through an access point.
It can provide Internet and email access without consuming cellular bandwidth.
Its range is limited by the local wireless network.

Wi-Fi calling can allow voice communication through Wi-Fi when cellular coverage
is poor or unavailable, but it depends on carrier and device support.

## Hotspot

A mobile hotspot shares a phone's cellular data connection with other devices.
Because Objective 1.2 already includes active-recall hotspot/tethering coverage,
Objective 1.3 keeps hotspot as study-guide coverage to avoid a duplicate
learning target.

Provider support, device support, data caps, and additional charges may affect
whether hotspot is practical.

## SIM and eSIM

A Subscriber Identity Module (SIM) identifies a cellular device to the carrier.
It can contain carrier and user information such as SIM identity, phone number,
cellular network information, and limited device-related storage.

A physical SIM is removable. An embedded SIM (eSIM) is built into the device and
is not physically removed. Many devices can support more than one SIM profile or
SIM option.

## Bluetooth pairing

Bluetooth pairing connects a nearby Bluetooth device, such as a headset or
watch, to a phone or tablet. The usual process is:

1. Enable Bluetooth on both devices.
2. Put the accessory into discoverable mode.
3. Select the discovered device.
4. Enter or confirm the PIN when required.
5. Test connectivity.

Manufacturers may require a specific key sequence or setup order, so service
documentation still matters.

## GPS and location services

GPS uses satellite timing information to determine location. Mobile devices also
use other location inputs, such as Wi-Fi and cellular towers, to support maps,
directions, and geotracking.

For troubleshooting, distinguish satellite-based GPS from broader “location
services,” which may combine several sources.
