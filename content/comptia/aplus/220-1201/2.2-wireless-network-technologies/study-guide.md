# Objective 2.2 study guide: Wireless Network Technologies

Objective 2.2 covers common wireless technologies, including 802.11 Wi-Fi,
Bluetooth, RFID, and NFC. The focus is recognizing what each technology is used
for and avoiding unsupported assumptions about vendor-specific behavior.

## Scope and source handling

CompTIA 220-1201 Objective 2.2 defines the scope. Professor Messer 220-1201
v1.70 pages 8–9 were used as private validation/reference pages only. This
guide uses original wording and does not reproduce the source diagrams, layouts,
or spectrum drawings.

The local environment did not provide a separate official CompTIA objectives
PDF. Objective 2.2 title and concept boundaries were validated against the
approved private reference and tracked against the CompTIA Objective 2.2 citation
in card metadata.

## 802.11 and Wi-Fi

802.11 is the IEEE wireless networking family used for Wi-Fi. IEEE standards
give vendors a common target so wireless devices and access points can
interoperate.

Wi-Fi standards are also referenced by generation names:

- 802.11ac maps to Wi-Fi 5.
- 802.11ax maps to Wi-Fi 6 and Wi-Fi 6E.
- 802.11be maps to Wi-Fi 7.

Wi-Fi commonly uses 2.4 GHz, 5 GHz, and 6 GHz spectrum. Some environments use
more than one band. Channels are groups of frequencies, and using non-overlapping
channels is preferable when planning nearby wireless networks.

Bandwidth describes how much frequency width is used by a wireless channel. The
approved source shows several channel widths, but OpenAPlus does not create
flashcards for every spectrum drawing or exact band endpoint in this draft.

## Bluetooth

Bluetooth is a short-range wireless technology used to remove cables from
nearby peripherals such as headsets, speakers, keyboards, and mice. It uses the
2.4 GHz range.

Bluetooth is useful for personal-area connections. Do not treat it as a direct
replacement for Wi-Fi infrastructure networking.

## RFID

Radio-frequency identification (RFID) is used to identify or track items such as
access badges, inventory, and animals. RFID systems transmit radio energy to a
tag and receive identifying information back. Some tag formats may have their
own power source.

## NFC

Near-field communication (NFC) is a short-range two-way wireless technology
built on RFID concepts. Common uses include payment systems, access tokens, and
identity-card style interactions.

NFC can also help bootstrap another wireless connection, such as assisting with
Bluetooth pairing.
