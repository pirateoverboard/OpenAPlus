# Objective 5.4 Interview Practice - Troubleshooting Mobile Devices

Use these prompts to practice spoken help desk answers. These are not regular
Anki cards and are not exported to TSV.

## Scenario: Rapid Battery Drain

Question:
A user says their phone battery suddenly drains before lunch. How would you
troubleshoot it?

Strong answer pattern:
I would ask when the drain started and whether anything changed, such as a new
app, OS update, travel, weak signal area, or new Bluetooth/GPS usage. I would
check battery-health and app-usage information, look for poor signal, disable
unneeded radios or features if appropriate, and avoid replacing the battery
until I have evidence that normal aging is the likely cause.

What to say first:
"I would start by finding out whether this is battery aging or a recent usage,
signal, or app behavior change."

Information to gather:
- When the drain started.
- Whether the device is often in a weak-signal area.
- Recent app installs or updates.
- Battery-health and app battery-usage evidence.

What to document:
- Battery-health status.
- Highest battery-usage apps.
- Signal or location pattern.
- Recommendation or escalation reason.

Mistakes to avoid:
- Replacing the battery without checking app usage or poor reception.
- Ignoring swollen-battery signs.

## Scenario: Swollen Battery

Question:
A user brings in a phone with a lifted screen and says the battery looks
swollen. What do you do?

Strong answer pattern:
I would stop normal troubleshooting, tell the user not to continue using or
charging the device, avoid opening or puncturing the battery, and follow the
organization's replacement and disposal process. I would document the physical
condition and escalate as a safety issue.

What to say first:
"I would treat that as a safety issue, not a normal battery-life complaint."

When to escalate:
Escalate immediately when the battery is swollen, leaking, overheating, or
physically unsafe.

Mistakes to avoid:
- Pressing the device closed.
- Charging the device to test it.
- Opening the battery pack.

## Scenario: Liquid Exposure

Question:
A user spilled a drink on a phone and wants to know whether you can test it
right away. How would you respond?

Strong answer pattern:
I would power the device down if it is still on, avoid charging it, avoid
pressing keys, remove removable items only if the design allows it, and let the
device dry before any power-on test. I would check for liquid-exposure evidence
and explain that powering it too soon can worsen damage.

What to say first:
"I would not plug it in or turn it on yet."

What to document:
- Time and type of exposure if known.
- Whether the device was powered or charging.
- Any Liquid Contact Indicator evidence.
- User data and backup status.

Mistakes to avoid:
- Charging the phone to see if it works.
- Heating the phone.
- Treating rice as a guaranteed repair.

## Scenario: Mobile App Will Not Install

Question:
A user cannot install a required app on a phone. How would you narrow the cause?

Strong answer pattern:
I would check available storage first if the error mentions space, then verify
network connectivity because the app must download from an external source. If
those are fine, I would check OS and app compatibility, then store cache where
applicable or account sign-in state depending on the platform and error.

What to say first:
"I would use the install error and device state to choose one likely category:
storage, network, compatibility, or account/store state."

Information to gather:
- Exact install error.
- Available storage.
- Network status.
- OS version and app requirements.
- Account or store sign-in status.

Mistakes to avoid:
- Factory-resetting before checking storage and connectivity.
- Assuming the app is broken without checking compatibility.

## Scenario: Stylus Not Working

Question:
A user says their tablet stylus stopped working during a meeting. What would
you check?

Strong answer pattern:
I would check whether the stylus has power or needs charging, then verify
Bluetooth pairing if the stylus uses it. I would inspect the stylus tip for
wear or damage and restart the device if the accessory and pairing look normal.

What to say first:
"I would separate stylus power, wireless pairing, physical tip condition, and
tablet state."

What to document:
- Stylus charge status.
- Pairing status.
- Any physical tip damage.
- Whether restart or re-pairing changed the symptom.

## Scenario: Slow Mobile Device

Question:
A user says their phone has become slow and apps crash. How would you explain
your troubleshooting plan?

Strong answer pattern:
I would check whether the OS and apps are up to date, verify available storage,
look for excessive background apps or high resource usage, and ask whether the
problem started after a specific app install or update. If reversible software
checks do not help, I would document the evidence and escalate for hardware or
replacement evaluation.

What to say first:
"I would start with reversible checks before treating the device as failed."

Mistakes to avoid:
- Jumping straight to replacement.
- Ignoring low storage.
- Leaving the ticket without documenting what changed after each test.
